## Why Start With a Cold Test

Before designing any study plan, we ran a full PET past paper with zero preparation.
The goal was not to see how well Nova could do — it was to find out *what kind of problem we were solving*.

A cold test produces honest data. It separates what a child genuinely knows
from what they've been drilled on. Without it, any study plan is guesswork.

## The Most Important Finding: Two Different Problems

The raw scores look like a general performance issue. They're not.

**Reading Part 4 (20%) and Listening Part 3 (0%)** share the same root cause:
both require the student to *build a search target before engaging with the material*,
then track that target actively. Nova processed both linearly and passively —
which causes total collapse on these task types regardless of language ability.

This is fixable in 1–2 weeks with framework installation.
It has nothing to do with vocabulary or listening stamina.

Compare this to a genuine ability gap: Reading Part 2 and Listening Part 2
both showed the pattern of *stopping at the first keyword match* without
verifying all conditions. That's a different mechanism — it requires
repeated practice with a deliberate verification step built in.

**The diagnostic principle that guided this analysis:**
> Strategy collapse and ability gap look identical in a score report.
> They require completely different interventions.
> Misreading one as the other is the most common mistake in exam prep.

---

## Three Cross-Cutting Error Patterns

**Pattern A — No pre-scan framework → strategy collapse**
Affects: Reading P4, Listening P3. Student enters task without establishing
what she's looking for. Result: passive, linear, reactive processing.
Fix: task-format training (1–2 weeks, not language work).

**Pattern B — Surface match → premature stop**
Affects: Reading P2, P3; Listening P2. First plausible answer terminates search.
Fix: drill with explicit verification protocol ("does this satisfy *all* conditions?").

**Pattern C — Long context > short context**
Listening P4 (100%) vs P2 (50%). Nova handles extended discourse well
because she can use context to build meaning. Short, high-density,
fast-switching items are harder. Fix: exposure over time, not a quick drill.

---

## The Counterintuitive Data Point

Listening Part 4: **6/6. Full marks.**

This is not luck. Part 4 is a long interview with complex questions —
conventionally considered one of the harder parts.
The score confirms that Nova's underlying listening comprehension is
significantly above her test score suggests.

Implication: the 15/25 total is not a listening ability problem.
It is almost entirely a task-format problem on Part 3.
Fixing Part 3 alone would likely push Listening to 19+/25 (Grade B territory).
